Case Study · UX Strategy · Cruise

Strategic Tactical
Journey

Embracing newcomers and welcoming returning explorers

Project Overview

This project aimed to educate users, new and old, about the value of a Viking cruise, while also showcasing the current offerings. For many users, understanding the full cruise experience can be a challenge — especially those new to a brand or who have never cruised before, resulting in lower conversion rates. As mobile usage grows, the solution also had to work seamlessly across all breakpoints.

Contribution

My Contribution

As the main designer on this project, I had full responsibility for ensuring the final product met all requirements. My focus was creating a user-centered design that provided an intuitive and seamless experience. This included conducting user research, creating wireframes, producing final UI designs, and building prototypes.

Problem

Problem

The existing design appeared outdated and lacked user appeal, potentially eroding trust. The absence of informative content hampered the user's understanding of the Viking cruise experience.

Goal

Goal

  • Create added value of the offer and the Viking experience
  • Optimize the urgency of the offer details
  • Increase booking conversions, with emphasis on new-to-brand users
  • Reduce bounce rate
Solution

Solution

  • Testimonials from satisfied customers — humanize the experience and showcase the unique value Viking offers
  • Inclusive Value — highlight that Viking includes no extra fees or hidden charges
  • User-Friendly Design — visually appealing, easy to navigate, aligned with Viking's brand identity
  • Educational Content — provide clients with insights into the cruise experience, itineraries, and information to help them make informed decisions
Process

Process

1

Kick Off with a Product Manager

Both myself and the product manager established the goal of the kick-off: to align on project requirements, objectives, and expectations. We reviewed user research findings, personas, customer insights, project scope, deliverables, timeline, technical constraints, and the KPIs we were aiming to hit.

2

Research

I analyzed the target user groups and their behaviors, conducted competitive and comparative research, and reviewed previous user interviews to inform the design process.

3

Sitemap

I mapped the landing page's full functionality — where CTAs led, what content would live where. Although there were slight deviations from the sitemap in the final design, it served as an invaluable north star.

4

Low-Fidelity Mocks

This is where everything starts to click. Low-fidelity mocks help the team and stakeholders visualize the product and align on direction. During this phase, we removed some sections, added new ones, and simplified others — for instance, the Discover Our Ships section was condensed into a highlight rather than a full section.

5

High-Fidelity Mocks

With UX finalized, I applied our design system and branding to produce pixel-perfect high-fidelity mockups across desktop, tablet, and mobile.

6

Testing

I set up a test on Usertesting.com to gather feedback before launch. Based on the results, I iterated on the design — for example, replacing a carousel arrow on the Itinerary Cards with a more visible "Show More" CTA button, which users had been missing.

Results

Conclusion

With this new version, users get a full experience. Rather than encountering an outdated page with no context, they're educated on the Viking brand, see the current deal clearly, and get a quick view of all available itineraries.
